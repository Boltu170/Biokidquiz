Question 4 Multiple Choice
What generates the high hydrostatic pressure needed for ultrafiltration in the glomerulus?
üìç Hint: Refer to Kidney Function - Ultrafiltration
‚úì Correct Answer: b) Contraction of the left ventricle and the afferent arteriole being wider than the efferent arteriole
üí° Explanation:
High hydrostatic pressure in the glomerulus is generated by two factors: (1) arteriole pressure from contraction of the left ventricle pumping blood, and (2) the afferent arteriole (incoming) being wider than the efferent arteriole (outgoing), which creates a bottleneck effect that increases pressure in the glomerular capillaries.
Question 5 Multiple Choice
Which of the following substances is NOT present in the glomerular filtrate under normal conditions?
üìç Hint: Refer to Kidney Function - Ultrafiltration (What's in the filtrate?)
‚úì Correct Answer: c) Large proteins (Mr > 69,000)
üí° Explanation:
Large proteins with molecular mass greater than 69,000, along with erythrocytes (red blood cells) and leucocytes (white blood cells), remain in the blood and are NOT filtered into the glomerular filtrate. The basement membrane acts as a filter that excludes these large molecules. Glucose, urea, amino acids, water, and small proteins do pass through into the filtrate.
Question 6 Fill in the Blank
In the proximal convoluted tubule, glucose is reabsorbed by __________ with sodium ions.
üìç Hint: Refer to Kidney Function - Selective Reabsorption in PCT
‚úì Correct Answer: co-transport (also accept: cotransport, symport)
üí° Explanation:
Glucose is reabsorbed from the PCT lumen by co-transport (also called symport) with sodium ions. The sodium-potassium pump actively removes Na+ from the epithelial cells, creating a concentration gradient. Na+ then moves down this gradient from the filtrate into the cell, and glucose is carried along with it through co-transport proteins. This is a form of secondary active transport.
Question 7 True/False

‚úì Correct Answer: True
üí° Explanation:

Question 8 Multiple Choice
What is the primary role of the loop of Henle?
üìç Hint: Refer to Kidney Function - Role of Loop of Henle
‚úì Correct Answer: b) To establish a lower water potential in the medulla tissue fluid, allowing water reabsorption from the collecting duct
üí° Explanation:
The loop of Henle's primary role is to create a water potential gradient in the medulla by actively transporting Na+ and Cl- ions out of the ascending limb. This lowers the water potential of the medulla tissue fluid, becoming increasingly negative deeper in the medulla. This gradient allows water to be reabsorbed by osmosis from the collecting duct as it passes through the medulla.
Question 9 Multiple Choice

‚úì Correct Answer: b) To produce more concentrated urine and conserve water in desert conditions
üí° Explanation:

Desert animals like the kangaroo rat have extremely long loops of Henle to produce very concentrated urine and minimize water loss‚Äîa crucial adaptation for survival in dry environments with limited water availability.
Question 10 Fill in the Blank
Osmoregulation is the regulation of the __________ __________ of the blood and body fluids.
üìç Hint: Refer to Homeostasis & Osmoregulation - Definitions
‚úì Correct Answer: water potential
üí° Explanation:
Osmoregulation is the regulation of the water potential of the blood and body fluids. It's a homeostatic mechanism controlled by negative feedback that maintains blood water potential within narrow limits. This is essential because cells need a constant water potential to function properly‚Äîtoo high or too low can damage cells through excessive water uptake or loss.
Question 11 Multiple Choice
Where are the osmoreceptors located that detect changes in blood water potential?
üìç Hint: Refer to Homeostasis & Osmoregulation - Control Mechanism
‚úì Correct Answer: b) Hypothalamus
üí° Explanation:
Osmoreceptors are located in the hypothalamus of the brain. These specialised receptors detect changes in the water potential of the blood. When blood water potential falls (becomes more negative/more concentrated), osmoreceptors stimulate neurosecretory cells to produce and release ADH. The hypothalamus is the control center, while the posterior pituitary gland stores and releases the ADH.
Question 12 True/False
True or False: When blood water potential decreases (blood becomes more concentrated), the posterior pituitary releases MORE ADH.
üìç Hint: Refer to Homeostasis & Osmoregulation - ADH Release Mechanism
‚úì Correct Answer: True
üí° Explanation:
When blood water potential decreases (blood is too concentrated/dehydrated), osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus detect this change and stimulate the posterior pituitary gland to release MORE ADH. This ADH increases the permeability of collecting duct walls to water, allowing more water reabsorption, producing concentrated urine, and raising blood water potential back to normal. This is negative feedback control.
Question 13 Multiple Choice
How does ADH increase the permeability of the collecting duct to water?
üìç Hint: Refer to Homeostasis & Osmoregulation - Effect of ADH
‚úì Correct Answer: b) By causing vesicles containing aquaporins (water channels) to fuse with the plasma membrane
üí° Explanation:
ADH binds to complementary receptors on the plasma membrane of cuboidal epithelial cells lining the collecting duct. Through signal transduction and activation of phosphorylase enzymes, this triggers vesicles containing aquaporins (water channel proteins) to fuse with the plasma membrane. This insertion of aquaporins increases membrane permeability to water, allowing more water to move by osmosis from the filtrate into the tissue fluid and blood.
Question 14 Fill in the Blank
ADH has a half-life of __________ minutes, meaning every 20 minutes about half the ADH in the blood is broken down.
üìç Hint: Refer to Homeostasis & Osmoregulation - Negative Feedback & ADH Breakdown
‚úì Correct Answer: 20
üí° Explanation:
ADH has a half-life of 20 minutes. This relatively short half-life is important for the negative feedback system‚Äîwhen blood water potential returns to normal, ADH release stops, and the existing ADH in the blood is gradually broken down. After 20 mins, 50% remains; after 40 mins, 25%; after 60 mins, 12.5%, etc. This allows the body to quickly respond to changing water balance conditions.
Question 15 True/False
True or False: In the distal convoluted tubule (DCT), the sodium-potassium pump actively removes Na+ from the filtrate into the blood and pumps K+ from blood into the filtrate.
üìç Hint: Refer to Kidney Function - Distal Convoluted Tubule
‚úì Correct Answer: True
üí° Explanation:
